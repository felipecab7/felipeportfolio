Full stack food delivery web app for 300 users






Overview
With the promise of delivering fresh authentic latin american food to University of Michigan students, Chichi’s quickly gained popularity on campus. This prompted its founders to look for ways to improve its delivery process, which was becoming a frustration to its customers as most of the interactions were happening through multiple applications (Snapchat, Text, and Venmo).

Problems
After conducting twenty interviews with primary and secondary users, I identified three main issues with the current process.

Overwelming
Chichis customers agreed that they are overwelmed by the amount of options and categories presented to them when using services like UberEats and DoorDash.
Unreliable
People unanimously agreed that food delivery services are Untrustworthy because of their unpredictable prices.
Uncertain
An overarching theme was that the inability to track progress throughout the purchase process.
Opportunity
How might we make Chichis' solution minimal, transparent, and reassuring?


Minimal
A solution should have few categories to choose from, revealing only the essentials in a progressive manner as users complete their purchase.
Transparent
Information such as delivery fees and source of food should be disclosed at the beggining and throughout the purchase process.
Reassuring
Chichis customers want to be certain about their purchase progress. It is important to keep them engaged by provinding them with constant feedback and having an open channel of communication thorughout the process.

Research Data

In this section, I give a more detailed explanation as to how I uncovered the design requirements mentioned above.

Step 1

The first step was to assess the current experience of Chichi’s customers and identify the causality of their behavior. I kicked off the research process with the following goals.

  • Identify what causes Chichi’s customers to pick up their phone and order Chichi’s.

  • Identify functional, emotional and social needs related to the core task they are trying to accomplish.

  • Identify Chichi’s customer’s desired outcomes associated with getting their core task done.

Interviews

I designed an interview protocol with mostly open-ended questions aimed at getting to the heart of the struggles Chichi’s customers face. I then conducted 20 user interviews via phone call with customers that had bought Chichi’s products within the last 2 months.

Affinity diagram

With the raw interview data at hand, I created sticky notes and consolidated quotes into main themes. Uncovering initial mental models.

Initial themes discovered

Journey Map

After gaining an initial understanding of the main needs and behaviors, I designed a map containing the journey a Chichis customer goes through from beginning to end and connected quotes and themes to each step of the process. This allowed me to visualize the mental models of users as the story unfolds and uncover not only functional needs but powerful emotional and social dimensions.

The middle section of the diagram contains Chichis customer journey from beginning to end. The light green bubbles represent quotes from the interviews classified as wants, desires, and gains; the darker green represents uncovered themes consolidated into emotions; and the black bubbles at the top represent measurable desired outcomes that can be used to build and measure possible solutions. When laying out the data this way, it is easier to dig deep into causality and find processes, struggles and unmet needs that could otherwise go unnoticed.

As an example, a frustration mentioned in the interviews was the inability to choose substitutes on the fly when choosing a product. This has to do with the way they currently communicate their orders to Chichis staff. Since Chichis customers are usually ordering as a group and orders are being handled through Snapchat and text, a member of the friend group asks everyone what they want to eat, and sends the order to Chichis. Since they have to wait until Chichis responds back (usually more than 15 minutes), when there is an item out of stock, the group member placing the order has to go back and ask his or her friends what substitutes they want and then go through the process of writing the order again. By digging deeper into the frustration of not being able to identify what items are out of stock I was able to uncover a deeper underserved need: being able to exchange information with friends easily when deciding what to order.

Findings

After gaining a deeper understanding of the mental model of Chichi’s customers I hypothesized that chichi’s customers are looking for solutions that:

  • Reduce time it takes to identify and select a product.

  • Increase ability to have control over purchase progress.

  • Increase ability to exchange information with friends when deciding what to order.

  • Increase ability to ensure ingredients are fresh and good quality.

  • Increase their ability to ensure delivery fees are predictable.

  • Minimize the time it takes to retrieve past orders.

Design and Ideation

Stage 01: Building empathy

As mentioned above, stories must be at the center of the product development process. Without believable characters, stories don't make an impact. Well-crafted personas act as the main characters in a movie and are the best way to build empathy among teams and make their struggles and desires memorable.

User persona including attitudinal, contextual, behavioral and personal data.

Stage 02: Digging deeper into desired outcomes

Desired outcome 1:

Reduce time it takes to identify and select a product.

Why is it important to her?

Anna is ordering Chichis after a long day. It's 1 am on a Thursday night, she has had a few drinks and the last thing she wants to do is to sorth thorugh long menus to decide what to eat.

Job Story:

"When deciding what to eat, I want to be exposed to as little information as possible so I can quickly make up my mind."

Criteria:

  • Progressive disclosure: Sequence information accross multiple interactions. By disclosing information progressively, revealing only the essentials, we are reducing the chances of Anna feeling overwelmed by the information she encounters.

  • Visual-search time: Reduce the time it takes to visually identify pieces of information Anna is looking for.

  • High familiarity: Favor recognition rather than recall. Following established conventions from other shopping websites helps Anna quickly build a mental model of how the interaction should work and make decisions without having to learn a new feature.

Desired outcome 2:

Increase ability to have control over purchase progress.

Why is it important to her?

One of Anna's biggest pain points is her inability to monitor her order status. Not being told that the order has been acknowledged or getting her order way past the stipulated estimate time of arrival makes her feel like she doesn't have control of her own time. She wants solutions that engage and empower her.

Job Story:

"When making an order, I want to have control over the purchase progress from selecting items to delivery so I can feel empowered and engaged."

Criteria:

  • Continuous feedback: Full and continuous feedback. Given Anna's low energy levels and her desire to be certain about the progress she is making throughout the purchase process, it is important to keep her engaged through constant feedback and continuous communication.

Stage 03: Generating ideas

The current Chichis menu is organized as follows: Combos, Empanadas, Goodies, drinks, and sauces. Each item can be bought separately except for the sauces. The initial brainstorming process helped me get a picture of how the current categories of the menu align with Anna's needs. This process raised key questions such as how items should be grouped and how she should go about selecting individual items as well as combos.

I tackled these questions using a semiformal notation called QOC (Questions, Options, and Criteria) which allowed me to visualize potential solutions to a question and compare them based on a set of criteria defined in stage 2. Progressive disclosure, high familiarity, visual-search time, and continuous feedback.

Q1: how to display products on the main page?

  • Option one (O1): Conventional approach with multiple categories shown at once on the main page. Having the user look at the categories (Combos, Empanadas, Drinks, Goodies) and scroll down is linked positively to High familiarity, but it's linked negatively to progressive disclosure (since all the menu items are presented at once), visual-search time (Anna has to scroll down to find items) and continuos feedback (there is no engagement or interaction that prompts feedback) 

  • Option two (O2): Linked positively to progressive disclosure (only two options are presented), visual search time (faster to identify two options as opposed to 5+), continuous feedback, and negatively to high familiarity (Since most food delivery sites display multiple categories at once Anna has to establish proficiency when encountering a new method).

Q2: how to display build your own and combo categories?

  • Option one (O1): A collapse menu feature would require Anna to go through two interactions, click on the title, and displaying the information within the category. It is linked positively to progressive disclosure (title appears first and items within category appear after she taps on the title, only the items of one category are displayed at a time), visual-search time (since only one category is displayed at a time, there is less scrolling, helping Anna find information faster) and prompt feedback (having to tap on each title keeps Anna engaged). The only negative assessment is its lack of familiarity.

  • Option two (O2): A list that resembles a conventional food delivery home screen with the two categories and its items below each title. It is linked negatively to progressive disclosure (all information is presented at once), prompt feedback (no interactions), and visual search time. It is linked positively to high familiarity (most food delivery sites display all their menu info at once).
