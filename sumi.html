Sumi

Skills

Design
Wireframes, prototypes, personas, scenarios, QOC

The Sumi prototype was the culmination of a semester-long project for SI 482 - Interaction Design Studio at the University of Michigan. We learned to define and narrow the scope of a problem, analyze the competitive landscape, empathize with users, ideate solutions and test them. The final deliverable was a working prototype supporting 15 - 30 user interactions taking place across 5-10 panels.

Role

I worked on the project with three other students. We all participated in the problem definition phase and competitive analysis. Once we started the ideation process, tasks were divided. I focused on transferring our design ideas into well-crafted paper prototypes and digital prototypes. I was also in charge of mapping out the interactions within the prototype using Invision.

Process

  • Defined a problem statement

  • Analyzed the competitive landscape

  • Developed relevant personas and scenarios

  • Design a solution framework using QOC assessment

  • Created low-fidelity wireframes based on QOC

  • Created paper prototypes to experiment with interactions

  • Conducted usability testing

  • Delivered working prototype using InVision


01 - Defining the problem

One of the requirements of the first stage of this project was to find a problem that adhered to the CHI 2019 student competition’s main theme: Weaving the Threads within the Social Fabric. We were encouraged to consider the ways that technology might be used to strengthen our social fabric --how individuals interact with each other within a community. We choose to tackle the problem many people face when making new year's resolutions: following through with those promises. We believed one of the reasons this happens is the lack of an accountability system where goals would be shared in a group setting and members would push each other to succeed.

02 - Competitive analysis

We kicked off the research phase by analyzing the competitive landscape of our problem. We identified four competitors.

  1. Coach.me: Direct competitor as it offers web and app interfaces to allow users to track habits and share it with friends or the community for accountability. The main tactic that is used to keep users accountable is to hire a personal certified coach who will guide them through meeting specific goals. Coach.me stresses that having just the habit tracker with progress reports and target reminders is helpful but the real accountability and productivity comes in with guidance from coaches. There is also a hidden feature that allows users to import their data to a CSV so that they can see which habits are really sticking and which aren’t. Something that the app does that we can consider for our product is using positive reinforcement instead of negative as Coach.me stresses building good habits instead of breaking bad ones.

  2. Find Your Ditto: An application that focuses on bringing people with chronic illnesses together. The positive aspects of this platform are that it allows users to find peers with similar experiences to chat with each other, either through online messaging or selecting/creating meetups in the app, and that users can only enter a community with an access code. The con of this app is that it does not allow any other sort of medium for communication, such as video or photo. We picked this app because it is a parallel competitor, which means it offers similar features to the same audience but does not directly compete against our product.

  3. Facebook Messenger: We picked Facebook Messenger to be our analogous competitor because we were inspired by the community of content sharing that the application evokes. Some positive features of Messenger are its accessibility over multiple platforms (mobile and desktop) and being able to utilize emojis, GIFs, and voice messages to portray emotions. A con with this platform is that it is not focused on meeting new users. We will attempt to use Messenger’s principles by encouraging positive content sharing that makes people feel good about their own goals as well as those of others. Omnifocus is a productivity app that allows users to keep track of tasks, deadlines, and projects. This is an indirect competitor because it offers a partial solution to our customer segment.   4. HabitShare: Direct competitor that allows users to share their habits with friends for extra accountability and motivation. Its best features include being able to customize who sees each habit you are tracking, creating custom goals, and being able to set custom reminders. HabitShare focuses on sharing habits with a user’s existing social network, while we want to give users the option to also find support outside of their social network.

From this analysis, our team learned about several applications that are diverse in user goals but all want to bring users together in the end. We also gained a better idea of what we want to implement and expand on in our own application. For instance, we liked how Coach.me utilizes positive reinforcement to encourage its users to build good habits instead of insisting them to break their bad ones. On the other hand, we admired HabitShare’s feature of sharing habits with a user’s existing social network, but we would like to expand on this idea by allowing users to create or find support groups with others that are outside of their existing social network. The competitive analysis process allowed us to scope out the current accountability app environment and develop a focus for how our product will be constructed.

03 - Personas and Scenarios

Kate’s background: Kate is a journalist with a background in copywriting and blogging. She also has experience working in public relations with multiple companies in Chicago. Katy loves storytelling and writing, so she keeps a journal where she does self-reflection and daily planning. Her passion for storytelling comes from her desire to bring communities closer. Katy has volunteered for multiple organizations since she graduated from college and plans to build her own non-profit in the near future.

Her story: As Katy is getting ready for work, she receives a notification from “the app”. Katy checks her phone and sees Sylvia, a member from her group, has sent a motivational message to everyone in the group reminding them to stay on track today. Katy cares for her health and wants to incorporate a variety of good habits into her daily routine, and she has decided she wants to start by cutting sugar out of her diet. After unsuccessfully trying to turn a sugar free diet into a habit with several health tracking apps, Katy has only cheated a few times since joining “the app” last month. Katy has never met Sylvia or the other members in her group. They were matched together because they opted to be in a group with strangers and have the same goals. At Katy’s work, it’s a co-worker’s birthday. Katy declines a piece of cake that’s being offered to her because she knows she’s going to have to check in with the group tonight on “the app”. In the evening, Katy receives a notification from the app reminding her to report her daily progress to her group. She likes being able to reflect on the decisions she made throughout the day and thrives off the positive feedback she receives from her group when she tells them she had another sugar free day

04 - Design Space Analysis

QOC: How should the group page be laid out?

Option Selected: List

We approached the design rationale with the goal of affording the user to choose the right group they want to look at in a quick manner. These are groups that the user has already chosen to be a part of for themselves. The list option follows strong convention with the text menu like look allowing the user to quickly scroll through no matter the screen size. Since the groups are distinguished in text in the list option, it allows the user to quickly recognize the group instead of having to recall it from memory. The tab option is convention for computer browsers but will be hard to translate for a smaller mobile screen. Although having the tab option would allow the user to choose between different groups and see the messages within the groups all in one step, it would be hard to have all the groups up at once without the tabs getting squished together up top. The square/icon look would be the most visually pleasing and just as easy to quickly scroll through as the list option but it doesn’t follow strong convention and would be hard to recognize off the bat with just an icon differentiating each group. Although all three had its pros, after further deliberation, we have decided to go with the list option.

QOC: How should we display progress?

Option Selected: ​​Circle Progress Bar

The goal of the progress bar is to help users track their advancement in their goals. They should be able to clearly visualize a pattern that is easy to understand. We selected the circle progress bar because we felt it would be a strong convention, show clear growth, and simple to visualize key milestones. While we found a line graph to easily track progress and visualize information, it was not a strong convention for what users see as a progress indicator. For a straight line as a progress bar, we thought it is good for showing growth and milestones, but it does not follow conventions as a measure of progress either.

QOC: How should we organize our content feed?

Option Selected: One Single Feed

The goal of our content feed is to provide a place for users to see content from their groups. They should be able to do tasks such as see likes on a message posted in a group, view a video, respond to a poll, and click on links without having to be on the group’s page itself. We chose the single feed option because we believe users would benefit from viewing content from all of their groups at once. Instead of only seeing the name of the group and having to click on it to access its information, users do not have to make any extra clicks and leave the page in a single feed. While a categorized feed is goal-driven, it is not easy to scan content from groups because the user has to open each individual chat to see what is being discussed.

05 - Paper prototype

After conduncting the QOC analysis and drawing intial mockups of the screens, we proceeded to create paper prototypes with two tasks in mind: create a group and join a group. This process allowed us to test the interactions before transitioning into a digital protoype.



We then conducted two usabiliy tests where we evaluated the successful completion of the two tasks mentioned above.

  • Observation: Users complained about not being able to add a category and a picture to each challenge.

  • Observation: Two users expressed the desire to have a more conventional dashboard where they could see their picture, select the challenges they belong to and create new challenges.

  • Observation: Users took more than 7 seconds deciding which option to choose from the pop-up window triggered after clicking on a challenge. Users expressed their desire to be directed to the challenge without having to deal with an extra menu.

Our high fidelity prototype was the culmination of weeks spent ideating, desining and iterating. We transitioned to digital after learning through mockups, wireframes and paper prototypes what impact our ideas would have on users. Thanks to the nature of rapid iteration, many of the wrong assumptions we had were discarded and that helped us move forward towards functionalities that based on data made a positive impact on users.
